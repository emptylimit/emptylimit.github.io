The Philosophy of Generalised Elements

Written by: Gabriel Field.

Introduction

Objects in an abstract category have no "elements" in the way that a set does. Nonetheless, pretending that they do can help develop intuition for various categorical constructions. How can we generalise the idea of an "element" of an object in $\mathbf{Set}$ to an arbitrary category?

Write $1 := \{0\}$ for the singleton set $1 \in \mathbf{Set}$. The elements $x \in X$ of a set $X$ correspond exactly to the functions $1 \to X$: $$\begin{align*} X &\simeq \mathbf{Set}(1, X) &&& \mathbf{Set}(1, X) &\simeq X \\ x &\mapsto (0 \mapsto x) &&& x &\mapsto x(0) \end{align*}$$ Abusing language, the elements of a set $X$ "are" the functions $1 \to X$. Since sets are completely determined by the elements they contain, these functions are enough to characterise a set. How does this generalise to other concerte categories?

Write $1 := \{e\}$ to be the one-element group. For an arbitrary group $G$, there is only one group homomorphism $1 \to G$; since homomorphisms must preserve the identity element, the only choice is the map $e \mapsto e_G$. So, the functions $1 \to G$ do not characterise the elements of the group $G$.

However, the functions $\mathbb{Z} \to G$ do characterise the elements of $G$. Writing $U(G)$ for the underlying set of $G$, we have the bijection $$\begin{align*} U(G) &\simeq \mathbf{Group}(\mathbb{Z}, G) &&&\mathbf{Group}(\mathbb{Z}, G) &\simeq U(G) \\ g &\mapsto (n \mapsto g^n) &&&g &\mapsto g(1) \end{align*}$$ so the elements of the group $G$ "are" the homomorphisms $\mathbb{Z} \to G$.

For a generic object $x$ in an arbitrary category $\mathsf{C}$, it is unclear what an "element of $x$" should even mean, let alone which object $e \in \mathsf{C}$ should represent them via the arrows $e \to x$. Instead, we take a more liberal approach. For any object $e \in \mathsf{C}$, the arrows $e \to x$ in $\mathsf{C}$ are known as the $e$-shaped elements of $x$, and $\bigcup_{e \in \mathsf{C}} \mathsf{C}(e, x)$ is known as the collection of generalised elements of $x$.

Further examples illuminate these ideas. Fix a simple undirected graph $G \in \mathbf{SimpleGraph}$. Then:

  • The graph $K_1 = K_1^c$ has one vertex and no edges. The $K_1$-shaped elements of $G$ are the vertices of $G$.
  • For $n \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$, the graph $K_n^c$ has $n$ vertices and no edges. The $K_n^c$-shaped elements of $G$ are pairs of $n$ vertices in $G$.
  • The graph $K_2$ has two vertices and one edge between them. The $K_2$-shaped elements of $G$ are the edges of $G$.
  • For $n \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$, the graph $K_n$ has $n$ vertices and each pair of distinct vertices is joined by an edge. The $K_n$-shaped elements of $G$ are the cliques of $G$.
  • For $n \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 1}$, the graph $P_n$ has $n$ vertices joined in a single path. The $P_n$-shaped elements of $G$ are the walks in $G$ of length $n$.
  • For $n \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 3}$, the graph $C_n$ has $n$ vertices joined in a single path. The $C_n$-shaped elements of $G$ are the cycles in $G$ of length $n$.

Working instead with graphs that need not be simple, we may have "degenerate" versions of the generalised elements listed above. For instance, the graph $G$ consisting of a single loop is terminal, and therefore has a $K_3$-shaped element (the degenerate "triangle" in $G$).

  • Fix $n \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$. Identify a topological space $E_n$ for which the $E_n$-shaped elements of $X$ are the pairs of $n$ (not necessarily distinct) elements of $X$.
  • What are the $[0, 1]$-shaped elements of $X$?
  • What are the $\mathbb{S}^1$-shaped elements of $X$? Here, $\mathbb{S}^1$ denotes the unit circle in $\mathbb{R}^2$ (with the subspace topology), which is homeomorphic to a quotient of $[0, 1]$ in which the endpoints $0$ and $1$ are identified.

Yoneda-style arguments

Sets are completely determined by their elements. Similarly, objects in a locally small category are determined by their generalised elements.

Lemma. (Generalised elements determine objects)

Fix a locally small category $\mathsf{C}$ and objects $x, y \in \mathsf{C}$. Then, $x \simeq y$ in $\mathsf{C}$ if and only if the functors $\mathsf{C}({-}, x), \mathsf{C}({-}, y) : \mathsf{C}^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathbf{Set}$ are naturally isomorphic.

This enables one to write "equational" proofs. The dual result establishes the "distributive law" $a \times (b + c) \simeq (a \times b) + (a \times c)$ in any Cartesian closed category $\mathsf{C}$, whenever the coproducts $b + c$ and $(a \times b) + (a \times c)$ exist: $$\begin{align*} \mathsf{C}(a \times (b + c), z) &\simeq \mathsf{C}(b + c, z^a) \\ &\simeq \mathsf{C}(b, z^a) \times \mathsf{C}(c, z^a) \\ &\simeq \mathsf{C}(a \times b, z) \times \mathsf{C}(a \times c, z) \\ \mathsf{C}(a \times (b + c), z) &\simeq \mathsf{C}( (a \times b) + (a \times c), z ) \end{align*}$$ Since each of the displayed isomorphisms is natural in $z \in \mathsf{C}$, we have that $\mathsf{C}(a \times (b + c), {-}) \simeq \mathsf{C}((a \times b) + (a \times c), {-})$ and hence $a \times (b + c) \simeq (a \times b) + (a \times c)$.
